\begin{abstract}
The long-only minimum variance portfolio is typically solved by forming a sample covariance matrix and passing it to a generic quadratic programming solver. This standard approach obscures the structure of the problem and incurs unnecessary computational and memory costs.

We show that the problem is more naturally expressed as a matrix-free least-squares problem on the returns matrix itself. This reformulation eliminates the need to form the covariance matrix and exposes a symmetric indefinite KKT system that can be solved efficiently using Krylov subspace methods. In particular, MINRES applied in a matrix-free fashion outperforms direct solves and generic optimisation routines, with speedups that grow with problem size.

A central insight of the paper is that covariance shrinkage, as introduced by Ledoit and Wolf, acts as a spectral preconditioner for the underlying linear system. This establishes a direct link between statistical estimation and numerical linear algebra: both operate by reshaping the eigenvalue spectrum of the same operator. Empirically, shrinkage significantly reduces Krylov iteration counts, providing computational gains alongside its statistical benefits.

The resulting perspective recasts minimum variance portfolio construction as a structured linear algebra problem rather than a generic optimisation task, enabling scalable solvers that are simple, fast, and well suited to large asset universes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The minimum variance portfolio is one of the most fundamental constructions in quantitative finance. Since \cite{markowitz1952}, it has been standard practice to estimate a covariance matrix from historical returns and solve the resulting quadratic program using a general-purpose optimisation routine.

This workflow is convenient but conceptually misaligned with the structure of the problem. The covariance matrix is not a primitive object; it is an estimate derived from a returns matrix. Once formed, this estimate obscures the underlying data structure and forces the use of dense linear algebra, even though all required operations can be expressed in terms of matrix-vector products on the returns themselves.

In this paper we take a different view. We show that the minimum variance problem is more naturally formulated as a least-squares problem on the returns matrix, avoiding the explicit construction of the covariance matrix altogether. This seemingly minor change has significant consequences. It exposes the problem as a structured linear system — specifically, a symmetric indefinite KKT system — that can be solved efficiently using Krylov subspace methods such as MINRES \cite{paige1975}.

This perspective leads to a hierarchy of solvers. At one end are generic optimisation frameworks, which are robust but computationally expensive. At the other are matrix-free Krylov methods, which exploit problem structure to achieve substantial gains in speed and scalability. The key computational advantage is that all operations reduce to repeated applications of the returns matrix and its transpose, avoiding both the $O(n^2)$ storage and the $O(n^2 T)$ cost associated with forming the covariance matrix.

A second contribution is conceptual. Covariance shrinkage, particularly the estimator of Ledoit and Wolf \cite{ledoit2004}, is usually motivated as a statistical device to improve estimation in high dimensions. We show that it can also be understood as a form of spectral preconditioning: by lifting small eigenvalues and compressing the spectrum, shrinkage directly improves the convergence behaviour of Krylov methods. In this view, \textbf{statistical regularisation and numerical preconditioning act on the same object} — the eigenvalue distribution of the system matrix — but for different reasons. Recognising this equivalence allows techniques from one domain to inform the other.

The resulting picture is simple but powerful. While the minimum variance problem is formally equivalent to a quadratic programme, its natural computational representation is linear algebraic — a structured matrix equation that admits fast, scalable solvers without the overhead of a general-purpose optimisation framework. This shift in perspective leads to implementations that are faster, more transparent, and better aligned with the statistical structure of the problem, particularly in large-scale settings where forming the covariance matrix is itself the dominant cost.

The closest related work applies GMRES to the MVP problem when the covariance matrix is singular \cite{bajeux2012}; our contribution is to avoid forming the covariance matrix altogether and to place the shrinkage–preconditioning duality at the centre of the analysis.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:problem} introduces the matrix-free formulation. Section~\ref{sec:kkt} derives the associated KKT system. Section~\ref{sec:solvers} presents Krylov-based solvers, including MINRES and a constraint-eliminated conjugate gradient method. Section~\ref{sec:precond} interprets Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage as spectral preconditioning. Section~\ref{sec:results} provides empirical results demonstrating the resulting performance gains.


\section{Problem Formulation}\label{sec:problem}

Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times n}$ be the matrix of daily returns for $n$ assets over $T$ trading days. The long-only minimum variance problem is:
\[
\min_{w} \; w^\top \Sigma w \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mathbf{1}^\top w = 1, \quad w \geq 0
\]
where $\Sigma = \frac{1}{T} X^\top X$ is the sample covariance matrix. Since $\Sigma \propto X^\top X$, this is equivalent to:
\[
\min_{w} \; \|Xw\|^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mathbf{1}^\top w = 1, \quad w \geq 0
\]
This reframing is key: the objective is now a squared norm of $Xw$, and we can evaluate $w \mapsto X^\top(Xw)$ using two matrix-vector products, without ever forming $X^\top X$.

\section{The KKT System}\label{sec:kkt}

Ignoring the inequality constraints for now, the Lagrangian for the equality-constrained problem is:
\[
\mathcal{L}(w, \lambda) = \|Xw\|^2 - \lambda(\mathbf{1}^\top w - 1)
\]
Setting derivatives to zero gives the stationarity condition $2X^\top X w = \lambda \mathbf{1}$. Combined with the constraint $\mathbf{1}^\top w = 1$, this yields the augmented KKT system:
\[
\begin{pmatrix} 2X^\top X & \mathbf{1} \\ \mathbf{1}^\top & 0 \end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix} w \\ \lambda \end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{0} \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}
\]
This $(n+1) \times (n+1)$ system is \textbf{symmetric but indefinite} — the zero in the bottom-right corner ensures the matrix has both positive and negative eigenvalues. This rules out standard CG, but it opens the door to MINRES.

The long-only constraint $w \geq 0$ is handled via an \textbf{active set method}: solve the KKT system on the current active set, drop any asset with a negative weight, and repeat. The process terminates in at most $n$ iterations in the worst case.

In practice the active set converges in 2--3 outer iterations when Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage is applied. There is a direct theoretical reason: the unconstrained minimum variance solution satisfies $w^\star \propto \Sigma_{\mathrm{LW}}^{-1}\mathbf{1}$. As the shrinkage intensity $\alpha$ increases, $\Sigma_{\mathrm{LW}} \to \alpha I$ and $w^\star \to \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}$, the equal-weight portfolio, whose components are all strictly positive. At any positive $\alpha$ the shrinkage regularises weights toward this interior point, so the unconstrained solution at the first solve typically has few or no negative components. Across 50 experiments with $n$ ranging from 100 to 1000 and $T = 2n$, the active set required exactly 2--3 outer iterations without exception, confirming that overall runtime is dominated by the linear solves rather than the outer loop.

\section{Krylov Solvers for the Indefinite System}\label{sec:solvers}

\subsection{CVXPY Reference Implementation}

Before developing the Krylov solvers we establish a reference solution using CVXPY \cite{diamond2016}, a domain-specific language for convex optimisation. The minimum variance problem maps directly onto a least-squares objective, which CVXPY dispatches to a general-purpose interior-point solver.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python]
import cvxpy as cp

n = R.shape[1]
w = cp.Variable(n)
problem = cp.Problem(
    cp.Minimize(cp.sum_squares(R @ w)),
    [cp.sum(w) == 1, w >= 0]
)
problem.solve(solver=cp.CLARABEL)
weights = w.value
\end{lstlisting}

The formulation is transparent and correct, and CVXPY handles the long-only constraint natively. We use the Clarabel interior-point solver \cite{clarabel2024}, which dispatches the problem as a second-order cone programme. Its cost is substantial: interior-point methods require $O(n^3)$ work per iteration, and the overhead of problem construction and solver communication adds further latency. At $n = 1000$ this amounts to roughly 5 seconds (Table~1), making CVXPY unsuitable for production use but ideal as a ground-truth reference.

\subsection{Direct Solve (Baseline)}

For small $n$, \texttt{numpy.linalg.solve} applied to the explicit KKT matrix is fast and exact. It requires forming $X^\top X$ — an $O(n^2 T)$ operation — but for $n \ll T$ this is not the bottleneck.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python]
def build_kkt(R):
    """Build the KKT system matrix and RHS for the
    minimum variance problem.

    Constructs the (N+1) x (N+1) indefinite KKT system for:

        min  w^T (R^T R) w
        s.t. sum(w) = 1

    The system has the form:

        [ 2 R^T R   1 ] [ w ]   [ 0 ]
        [ 1^T       0 ] [lam] = [ 1 ]
    """
    n = R.shape[1]
    A = np.zeros((n + 1, n + 1))
    A[:n, :n] = 2 * R.T @ R        # form X^T X explicitly
    A[:n, n] = 1                   # budget constraint column
    A[n, :n] = 1                   # budget constraint row
    b = np.zeros(n + 1);  b[n] = 1
    return A, b

active = np.ones(n, dtype=bool)
while True:
    A, b = build_kkt(R[:, active])
    sol = np.linalg.solve(A, b)
    w_a = sol[:active.sum()]
    if np.all(w_a >= 0):
        break
    active[np.where(active)[0][w_a < 0]] = False
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{MINRES}

MINRES \cite{paige1975} is a Krylov method for symmetric indefinite systems. It minimises the residual $\|Ax - b\|$ over the Krylov subspace $\mathcal{K}_k(A, b)$. Applied to our KKT system, the key ingredient is a matrix-free \texttt{LinearOperator} that applies the KKT matrix via two matrix-vector products on the returns matrix — $X^\top X$ is never formed.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python]
from scipy.sparse.linalg import LinearOperator, minres

def matvec(x):
    out = np.empty(n + 1)
    out[:n] = 2.0 * (R.T @ (R @ x[:n])) + x[n]
    out[n]  = x[:n].sum()
    return out

kkt  = LinearOperator(shape=(n+1, n+1), matvec=matvec)
b    = np.zeros(n + 1);  b[n] = 1.0
w, _ = minres(kkt, b)
\end{lstlisting}

Convergence depends on the eigenvalue distribution of the KKT matrix. For well-conditioned problems MINRES converges in very few iterations; Section~\ref{sec:precond} discusses how Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage compresses the spectrum and accelerates convergence in the ill-conditioned case.

\subsection{Constraint-Eliminated CG}

A cleaner approach avoids the indefinite system entirely. We parameterise the solution as:
\[
w = w_0 + Pv
\]
where $w_0 = \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}$ is any particular solution satisfying $\mathbf{1}^\top w_0 = 1$, and $P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times (n-1)}$ is an orthonormal basis for the null space of $\mathbf{1}^\top$, obtained via QR factorisation of $\mathbf{1}$.

Any choice of $v$ gives a feasible $w$. The reduced unconstrained problem in $v$ is:
\[
\min_v \; \|Xw_0 + XPv\|^2
\]
with normal equations:
\[
(XP)^\top (XP) \, v = -(XP)^\top (Xw_0)
\]
The matrix $(XP)^\top(XP)$ is \textbf{symmetric positive definite}, so standard CG applies. The matrix-vector product $v \mapsto P^\top X^\top (X(Pv))$ requires four cheap operations and never forms $X^\top X$ or $(XP)^\top(XP)$ explicitly. In the implementation $P$ is never stored: it is applied via an implicit Householder reflector in $O(n)$ time.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=python]
from scipy.sparse.linalg import LinearOperator, cg

# Implicit null-space basis via Householder reflector
sqrt_n = np.sqrt(float(n))
beta   = 1.0 / (n + sqrt_n)
v0     = 1.0 + sqrt_n

def P_apply(y):                     # P y  (n-1 -> n)
    s = y.sum()
    out = np.empty(n);
    out[0] = -beta * v0 * s;
    out[1:] = y - beta * s
    return out

def PT_apply(u):                    # P^T u  (n -> n-1)
    s = v0 * u[0] + u[1:].sum()
    return u[1:] - beta * s

w0  = np.ones(n) / n                # particular solution: 1^T w0 = 1
rhs = -PT_apply(R.T @ (R @ w0))

def matvec(y):
    return PT_apply(R.T @ (R @ P_apply(y)))

A_red = LinearOperator(shape=(n-1, n-1), matvec=matvec)
v, _  = cg(A_red, rhs)
w     = w0 + P_apply(v)                # feasible: 1^T w = 1
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Which solver to use}

MINRES is the recommended default: it operates directly on the symmetric indefinite KKT system, requires no constraint elimination, and converges in fewer iterations than CG across all settings tested. The constraint-eliminated CG is mathematically cleaner — it solves a symmetric positive definite system of size $n-1$, for which standard CG theory applies without qualification — and is preferable when the returns matrix $X$ is sparse (where applying $X$ and $X^\top$ is cheap but materialising $X^\top X$ is not), or when a positive definite structure is required for compatibility with preconditioners designed for SPD systems. For dense problems with Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage, MINRES is faster and simpler to apply.

\section{Ledoit-Wolf Shrinkage}
\label{sec:precond}

The sample covariance matrix $\frac{1}{T}X^\top X$ is a poor estimator when $n$ is large relative to $T$: its smallest eigenvalues are severely downward-biased, and the eigenvalue spread — the ratio of largest to smallest — far exceeds that of the true covariance. Ledoit and Wolf \cite{ledoit2004} propose replacing the sample covariance with a convex combination of the sample estimate and the identity:
\[
\Sigma_{\text{LW}} = (1-\alpha) \cdot \frac{X^\top X}{T} + \alpha I
\]
The shrinkage intensity $\alpha \in (0,1)$ is chosen analytically to minimise the expected Frobenius distance to the true covariance, requiring no cross-validation. The effect on the spectrum is direct: adding $\alpha I$ lifts every eigenvalue by $\alpha$, bounding the smallest away from zero and compressing the overall spread. The condition number satisfies:
\[
\kappa(\Sigma_{\text{LW}}) \leq \frac{\lambda_{\max} + \alpha}{\alpha}
\]
and is fully controlled by $\alpha$. Subsequent work by the same authors provides an analytical nonlinear shrinkage formula \cite{ledoit2020} that applies a different shrinkage coefficient to each sample eigenvalue, achieving a strictly smaller Frobenius loss when $n/T$ is not negligible.

In the implementation, shrinkage is applied by augmenting the return matrix rather than forming $\Sigma_{\text{LW}}$ explicitly. Setting $c = 1 - \alpha$ and $\gamma = \alpha \cdot \|\Sigma\|_F / n$, the stacked matrix $\tilde{X} = [\sqrt{c}\,X;\,\sqrt{\gamma}\,I]$ satisfies $\tilde{X}^\top \tilde{X} = c\,X^\top X + \gamma I \propto \Sigma_{\text{LW}}$, so any of the solvers above can be applied to $\tilde{X}$ without modification.

From the perspective of numerical linear algebra, compressing the eigenvalue spectrum of the system matrix is precisely what a preconditioner does. Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage therefore plays a dual role: it reduces statistical estimation error and, as a side effect, accelerates iterative solvers by clustering the eigenvalues. The two motivations are independent but arrive at the same modified matrix.

This observation opens a richer design space. The identity shrinkage target $\alpha I$ is the simplest choice, but the preconditioning literature offers more powerful alternatives. A factor-model preconditioner — replacing $\alpha I$ with a low-rank-plus-diagonal approximation $BB^\top + D$ — would cluster eigenvalues more tightly at the cost of a slightly more expensive application. Incomplete Cholesky factorisation of the shrunk matrix provides a general-purpose option. For rolling-window problems where the covariance changes slowly between periods, a preconditioner built from the previous period's factorisation could be reused with minimal update cost. We leave the exploration of these structured preconditioners to future work.

The impact of Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage on the Krylov solvers is immediate: MINRES iterations drop from 39 to 16 and CG from 80 to 26, reflecting the compressed eigenvalue spectrum of the regularised system. The KKT direct solve is unaffected — it does not iterate — and its runtime stays flat across both panels because forming $X^\top X$ dominates regardless of conditioning.

MINRES is the fastest solver in both settings. At $n = 1000$ it is roughly 4$\times$ faster than KKT direct because it avoids the $O(n^2 T)$ cost of assembling $X^\top X$ explicitly. The constraint-eliminated CG is close behind and is the natural choice when $X$ is sparse or when memory for $X^\top X$ is unavailable.

Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} extends these results across problem sizes and conditioning regimes. Panel~(a) shows wall-clock time as a function of $n$ (with $T = 2n$ and Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage throughout). The KKT direct solve scales as $O(n^3)$ — dominated by the $n \times n$ linear system — while MINRES and CG grow substantially more slowly, with the gap widening visibly beyond $n \approx 300$. Panel~(b) fixes $n = T = 800$ (a mildly ill-conditioned regime with $n/T = 1$) and varies the shrinkage intensity $\alpha$, tracing out the full range of condition numbers. Both solvers require more iterations as $\kappa$ grows, and CG is consistently more sensitive to conditioning than MINRES. At low $\kappa$ (heavy shrinkage) both methods converge rapidly; the practical operating point for most asset universes sits in this low-$\kappa$ regime.

\section{Conclusion}

We have shown that the long-only minimum variance portfolio problem, when formulated directly in terms of the returns matrix, is a structured linear algebra problem that admits a clean hierarchy of solvers. Avoiding the explicit formation of the sample covariance matrix is both numerically advantageous and algorithmically revealing: it exposes the KKT system's symmetric indefinite structure and opens the door to Krylov methods that scale to large universes.

The key empirical finding is that MINRES, applied matrix-free to the KKT system, is faster than a direct solve by a factor of roughly four at $n = 1000$ and the gap widens as $n$ grows. Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage \cite{ledoit2004} compresses the eigenvalue spectrum of the system, reducing MINRES iterations from 39 to 16 and CG iterations from 80 to 26 in our experiments. The shrinkage intensity is chosen to minimise estimation error — the acceleration of the iterative solver is a consequence, not a design goal.

The paper also draws a connection that the two literatures involved — numerical linear algebra and high-dimensional statistics — do not usually make explicit. The shrinkage estimator of Ledoit and Wolf and the preconditioning theory of Golub and Van Loan \cite{golub2013} address different problems but act on the same object: the eigenvalue spectrum of the system matrix. Recognising this duality suggests that advances in one field transfer directly to the other, and points toward richer preconditioners — factor-model or low-rank structured — as a natural next step.

Once formulated in this way, the widespread reliance on covariance-based QP solvers appears more as a historical artifact than a necessity.
